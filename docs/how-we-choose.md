# How we choose

## No affiliates, no sponsors, no paid placement

Nobody can buy a listing here. There are no affiliate links, no sponsorship deals, and no referral fees anywhere in this directory. If a tool appears, it is because it earned the spot on its own merits, and if it stops earning that spot it comes out, regardless of who makes it.

Practically, that means every link in this directory pays us nothing when you click it or sign up. Removing a tool costs us nothing either, no contract to unwind, no revenue to protect. That is the only way a "best free tools" list can be trusted: if the person compiling it has nothing riding on which tool you pick.

## What gets a tool listed

A tool earns a place here on four tests. It needs a genuinely useful free tier, not a fourteen-day trial dressed up as one. A UK small business needs to be able to actually deploy it, not a product aimed at enterprises or a different market. It has to have been used on real client work, not just read about. And it needs credible alternatives, because a tool with no substitute is a lock-in risk, not a recommendation.

Tools that fail any of those tests do not get added, however well known they are.

## Why every tool shows alternatives

Every entry in this directory carries at least two alternatives, including open source or self-hosted options wherever a credible one exists. This is deliberate: the point of the directory is to stop small businesses being funnelled towards a single vendor, which is exactly what most "best free tools" content does under the guise of a recommendation.

The only exception is a statutory body with no substitute, such as the ICO as the UK's data protection regulator. Everywhere else, if we cannot name a genuine alternative, that is a gap in the listing, not a reason to drop the requirement.

## How the value figure works

Each tool carries an honest annual value: what you would actually pay a commercial provider for the same capability, in pounds. It is never the price of the tool's own paid tier, and it is never inflated to make the total look more impressive. If a free tool only replaces a box you would have ticked anyway, the figure is low or zero. The whole point is that the total has to survive a reader who does not trust "free tools" lists, because most of them do not deserve trust.

These figures get challenged and revised. When a price changes, or when we get it wrong, we correct it. Every tool also shows a "last verified" date, so you can see how recently the figure and the free-tier claim behind it were actually checked, rather than taking either on faith.

## What the free tier really includes

For every tool we state, in plain English, what the free tier genuinely includes and exactly where it stops: the user limit, the storage cap, the feature gate, whatever the real edge is. This exists because most "free" claims quietly hide a cliff edge that only appears once you have built your business around the tool. That is the exact problem this directory exists to fix, so pretending it does not exist here would defeat the point.

## When we say build it yourself

For a genuinely single-purpose need, a booking form, a simple contact tracker, a links page, a small bespoke build is now a realistic option with AI-assisted development, and where that is true we say so plainly.

We do not say it for anything security, legal, accounting or deliverability critical. Those products have spent years debugging edge cases you have never thought about, and taking that on yourself is a real risk, not a saving. Where we recommend the established tool over a DIY build, that is the reason.

## Retiring tools

Tools that stop being free, or simply stop being good, are archived, never silently deleted. Old client links keep working: anyone who received a stack containing a retired tool still sees it, marked clearly as no longer recommended, pointing at its alternatives instead of vanishing without explanation. Honesty about what changed matters as much as honesty about what is still current.
